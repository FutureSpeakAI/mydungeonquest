# Frame-instrument briefs vs painter habits

**The rule.** When a correctly-calibrated instrument keeps refusing work that
looks honest, view the flagged plates with eyes before touching the
instrument OR re-rolling: the calibration *brief* may be the liar. Design
fixture briefs to be honest to the painter's *observed* habits, and aim
constancy questions at identity, never staging.

**Why (three proven classes from one series):**
1. A one-soul brief whose mood begged company ("dusk trade weighed…") plus a
   companionship clause got a painted counterparty added 2/2 independent
   mints — the closure court refused rightly; the cure was naming the second
   soul in the brief, making the painter's habit lawful.
2. Canon micro-attributes the mood never ordered ("bolted to the gate arch")
   rendered 0/4 even when explicitly ordered once — image models resist
   physically-odd attachments; identity attributes (kind, material, scale,
   twin pans) rendered 4/4.
3. Independent mints of one brief NEVER converge staging (mounting, angle,
   position, dressing) — cache-key independence guarantees variance by
   design. A constancy question that demands staging convergence fails all
   honest work forever: a court that cannot pass honest work is decoration
   in the mirror, exactly as one that cannot fail is.

**How to apply:**
- Calibration known-goods must be briefs the painter passes at high
  observed rate; known-bads stay deterministic COUNT/ABSENCE lies (a
  reference-less judge cannot verify identity — never build identity
  crossings as controls).
- Constancy/fixture questions: demand the canon's kind, material, scale,
  distinctive form — declare staging variance lawful in the question text
  itself; refuse only missing/different/unrecognizable.
- Sealed-question files carry a protocol tag as a verdict-cache key
  ingredient: bumping the tag is the sanctioned freshener after any
  question amendment — and EVERY call-site pin (sha pin AND protocol-tag
  pin) moves in lockstep, or an honest court refuses at 7ms.

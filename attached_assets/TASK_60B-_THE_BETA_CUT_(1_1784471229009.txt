SECTION 1: STAGE ONE, THE TRUE IMAGE
1. THE REFERENCE SHEET LAW. Every carded subject earns exactly one composite reference sheet: souls, places, species, and notable items, where a notable item is a named thing the tale features rather than every ladle in the trove. A sheet is a single image in a fixed grid with a fixed cell order: the face, the profile, the full figure in current costume, and the defining detail. Sheets are minted once at introduction, derived by edit-mode operations from the sealed anchor rather than fresh rolls, with canonical cells at neutral pose, eye level, and flat lighting; minting prompts carry the silence clause so no sheet bears labels or lettering of any kind. A sheet is attested by hash, judged by the sheet court before first use, and re-minted only when canon lawfully changes through an operation, the prior sheet remaining attested for every plate it grounded. Plates ground on the original sheet forever; generations are never chained, so cumulative drift has no mechanism. A directive-sanctioned re-anchor lever exists for a tale whose constancy yellows climb, and it defaults off.
2. THE SLOT LAW. Reference budgets are pinned constants per provider, and for the Gemini painter they are five identifiable subjects and fourteen total reference images per call. Selection is deterministic: the principal's sheet always rides, the ground's place sheet always rides, remaining cue subjects follow in cue order, one species sheet covers every instance of that species, fixtures ride the place sheet and never spend a slot, and items ride as sealed canon text unless this turn's prose features a notable item, which then earns its sheet a slot. Every attached sheet gets a binding line in the prompt stating in plain words which subject it depicts exactly, in deterministic order, byte-stable. The validator enforces the identity ceiling on the cue itself, and the Art Director overflows by direction, composing shots that do not need every face, so the model is never asked to hold more likenesses than the pinned budget.
3. THE FRESH PLATE LAW. Every rendered plate is bound by attestation to the turn it illustrates, and the render door refuses a plate without this-turn papers. A failed paint yields an honest empty frame with its status named, never a recycled image, and no code path may reuse a prior turn's plate as the current turn's illustration. The Art Director's brief is a storyboard panel of the sealed prose: framing, angle, blocking, and light chosen from the moment, under a shot grammar the directive states in general language.
4. THE PROP LAW. An item may appear in a plate only if its holder stands in the cue, it is a fixture of the current ground, or this turn's operations moved it. The validator enforces this on the cue's item list, which ends the wandering lantern and the ubiquitous bell at the door rather than at the easel.
5. THE VERTICAL LAW. Plates are vertical by default at one pinned portrait ratio chosen from the provider's native set, subjects held toward center, the ratio checked deterministically on delivery, and the feed frame rebuilt to letterbox rather than crop, so a vertical plate renders whole on a phone.
6. THE EASEL PRIORITY. The paint request enqueues the moment its cue seals, ahead of voice and music, and the queue order is deterministic and gated.
7. THE MENDED PLATE CLAUSE. The production warden's second attempt may be a targeted edit of the failed plate that preserves composition while removing the offense; the proving harvest's mint ladders remain fresh-dice as pinned, and no floor or bar moves.
